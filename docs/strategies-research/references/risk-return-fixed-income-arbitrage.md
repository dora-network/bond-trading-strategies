# Risk and Return in Fixed-Income Arbitrage

**URL:** https://www.anderson.ucla.edu/documents/areas/fac/finance/769.pdf

**Authors:** Duarte, Longstaff, Yu (UCLA Anderson)

## What It Covers

Comprehensive study of five widely used fixed-income arbitrage strategies: swap spread, yield curve, mortgage, volatility, and capital structure arbitrage. Constructs return indexes by following specific trading strategies through time.

## Key Claims

1. **All five strategies generate positive excess returns** on average, after realistic transaction costs *(extracted)*
2. **Returns are positively skewed** — strategies generate larger offsetting positive returns than negative ones, contradicting the "picking up nickels in front of a steamroller" narrative *(extracted)*
3. **Swap spread arbitrage:** Long par swap (receive fixed, pay floating LIBOR) + short par Treasury bond, betting the fixed annuity received exceeds the floating spread paid *(extracted)*
4. **Yield curve arbitrage:** Long and short positions at different yield curve points, typically butterfly trades that neutralize level and slope exposure, exploiting curvature mispricing *(extracted)*
5. **Yield curve trades require less capital** than swap spread strategies for the same volatility target — better natural hedging *(extracted)*
6. **Swap spread is not pure arbitrage** — exposed to indirect default risk through LIBOR rates during banking stress *(extracted)*
7. **Strategy-specific risk characteristics** differ substantially; combining strategies provides diversification benefits *(inferred)*

## Limitations

- Study predates GFC and post-2008 regulatory changes
- Does not account for the structural decline in dealer balance sheet capacity
- Return indexes are simulated, not from actual hedge fund returns
