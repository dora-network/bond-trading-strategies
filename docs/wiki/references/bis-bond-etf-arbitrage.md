# BIS: Anatomy of Bond ETF Arbitrage

**URL:** https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt2103d.htm

**Author:** Karamfil Todorov (BIS)

## What It Covers

Analysis of the unique arbitrage mechanism for bond ETFs: how creation/redemption baskets differ from holdings, why baskets change daily, and how this decoupling affects market stability.

## Key Claims

1. **Creation/redemption baskets are only a small subset of holdings** — less than 3% for the largest bond ETF; equity ETF baskets are almost identical to holdings *(extracted)*
2. **Creation baskets tend to have longer duration and higher liquidity** than redemption baskets *(extracted)*
3. **3-5 active APs per ETF** — APs can use creations/redemptions to manage their own bond inventory, not just for arbitrage *(extracted)*
4. **Decoupling of baskets from holdings weakens arbitrage forces** but allows ETFs to absorb bond market shocks *(extracted)*
5. **During March-April 2020 stress**, ETFs traded at discounts while redeeming baskets more illiquid than holdings — a shock absorption mechanism *(extracted)*
6. **Flexible baskets allow diverse APs** to participate, improving primary market efficiency *(extracted)*
7. **Partial convertibility prevents fire sales** — in-kind redemptions absorb selling pressure without forced bond market sales *(extracted)*

## Implications for Continuous Bond Markets

- **ETF-like fractional trading already provides a window** into how continuous bond markets behave
- **The decoupling of baskets from NAV** shows that arbitrage is never "perfect" even with continuous trading — market structure matters more than trading frequency
- **AP arbitrage 4-10 minute correction time** (SSRN study) suggests real-time continuous trading still has meaningful latency in price discovery
- **Fractionalization without in-kind creation/redemption** (pure token market) could be LESS stable than ETF structure

## Limitations

- Covers only ETF structure, not direct bond tokenization
- BIS perspective — may emphasize systemic stability concerns
